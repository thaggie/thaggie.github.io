Key dates and events

1497

John Cabot (Italian immigrant to England) explored and mapped Canada's East coast.

1534 — 1542

Jacques Cartier made three voyages across the Atlantic, claimed the land for France, heard the Iroquos say “kanata”, meaning village.

1550s

Canada began appearing on maps.

1604

Pierre de Monts and Samuel de Champlain estabish first European settlement North of Florida, in Canada Port-Royal was established in what is now Nova Scotia.

French colonists began to settle the maritime provinces, the “Acadians”.

1608

Samuel de Champlain built a fortress at what is now Québec City. Allied with Algonquin, Montagnais, and Huron against the Iroquois.

The French and Aboriginal people collaborated in the fur trade.

1610

English settlement began.

1670

King Charles the II of England granted the Hudson's Bay company the trading rights over the watershed draining into Hudson's Bay.

1690

My only reply will be from the mouths of my cannons!

Count Frontenac

1700s

French Canadians started using the maple leaf as symbol.

1701

French made peace with the Iroquois.

1755 — 1763

War between Britain and France uprooted two-thirds of Acadians in the “Great Upheaval”.

1758

First representive assembly elected in Halifax, Nova Scotia.

1759

The British defeated the French in the Battle of the Plains of Abraham at Québec City.

1763

Territorial rights for aboriginal people rights were first guaranteed through the Royal Proclamation of 1763 by King George III.

1773

Second representive assembly elected in Prince Edward Island.

1774

British Parliament passed the Quebec Act. One of the consitutional foundations of Canada. Allowed religious freedom for Catholics and permitted them to hold public office. The Quebec Act restored French civil law while maintaining British criminal law.

1775

American military invasion of Quebec, defeated by Sir Guy Carleton (Lord Dorchester).

1776

Declaration of independence forming the United States. 40,000 “loyalists” fled to Nova Scotia and Quebec.

1782 — 1783

Sir Guy Carleton (Lord Dorchester), supervised the loyalist migration to Nova Scotia and Quebec.

1791

The Constitutional Act divided the province of Quebec into upper (Ontario, anglophones, protestant) and lower (Quebec, francophones, catholic) Canada.

The Canadas had legislative assemblies elected by the people and where collectively known as “British North America”.

1792

Some black Nova Scotians establish Freetown, Sierra Leone (West Africa) a new British colony for freed slaves.

1793

Upper Canada, led by Lieutenant Governor John Graves Simcoe, became the first province in the British Empire to move toward the abolition of slavery.

1840

Random facts

Aboriginal people are 3 distinct groups: Indian (65%), Métis (40%) and Inuit (4%, “the people”).

There are about 600 First Nations communities on reserve land, about half of First Nations people live in cities.

Languages

  • 18 million anglophones
  • 7 million francophones
  • New Brunswick is the only officially bilingual province.
  • In Vancouver, 13% of the population speak Chinese languages at home.
  • In Toronto, 7% speak Chinese languages at home.

First Nation Lifestyles

  • The Huron-Wendat of the Great Lakes region, like the Iroquois, were farmers and hunters.
  • The Cree and Dene of the Northwest were hunter-gatherers.
  • The Sioux were nomadic, following the bison (buffalo) herd.
  • The Inuit lived off Arctic wildlife.
  • West Coast natives preserved fish by drying and smoking.
